## Workflow Explainer: More Cards Did Not Mean More Clarity

A 160-second workflow video needed frequent feedback, but the working rule was still one primary card and one optional keyword group at a time.

What worked:

- A new visual response every 4-7 seconds for a long explainer
- Cards with low information per card
- Stable subtitles and protected face area
- A sequence of dashboard, warning, process, review, and final-thesis cards

Lesson:

Increase semantic variation and pacing, not simultaneous density.

## Hot-Topic Format: Proof, Type, Diagram, Judgment

The strongest reusable rhythm came from alternating:

1. Giant unresolved judgment
2. One stable central object or concept map
3. Real recording in a framed proof panel
4. Two- or three-point explanation
5. Final recommendation

What worked:

- Real recordings established trust without carrying the whole video.
- Giant Chinese words carried the hook.
- Diagrams explained relationships.
- The final frame produced a screenshot-worthy judgment.

Watch item:

The final giant line and subtitle can duplicate each other. Suppress or shorten the subtitle when the final type already carries the sentence.

## LongCat: B-Roll Went From Too Long To Too Much To Selective

First issue:

- Low-information stock shots held for 4-6 seconds made the video feel generic.

Over-correction:

- Short-fast-many cutting on nearly every sentence forced constant reorientation and weakened the speaker.

Final rule:

- Keep hook, skepticism, jokes, complex explanation, personal judgment, and ending on A-track.
- Use B-roll bursts only for dense specs, concrete nouns, infrastructure, numbers, and comparisons.
- Within selected bursts, vary shot lengths from about 0.45 to 1.25 seconds.
- Return to A-track after each burst.
- Keep at least half the runtime uninterrupted; ordinary target is 55% or more.

## Generated B-Roll: Semantic Fit Beats Quantity

Problems observed:

- Reusing the same generated clip at several unrelated points felt cheap.
- Fake UI-like generations threatened the proof boundary.
- Abstract clips could not be rescued by adding labels.

Final rule:

- Use each generated or stock clip once by default.
- Keep generated media for abstract mechanisms, infrastructure, or strong metaphors.
- Reject weak semantic matches at the asset stage.
- Keep API tests, named platform behavior, and final claims on A-track or real proof.
- A missing B-roll shot is better than misleading filler.

## Vertical Reasonix: Desktop-Scale Cards Look Weak On Mobile

Initial vertical review found:

- A-track tags and current-beat cards were too small and high.
- B-track metrics and cards did not occupy enough of the vertical frame.
- Bilingual subtitles sat too close to the bottom controls.

Fixes:

- Increase vertical card and metric scale.
- Lower visual anchors into useful viewing zones while keeping face safety.
- Raise subtitles.
- Use short full-screen B-track on metrics, setup steps, mechanisms, and verdicts.

Lesson:

Do not adapt 16:9 by merely stacking or shrinking. Recompose the hierarchy for phone distance.

## Horizontal Breathing Layout: Right Rail Became Too Heavy

Initial 16:9 design concentrated B-roll, card, shadow, and keywords on the right, creating a second dominant subject.

Fixes:

- Reduce right-panel width, opacity, shadow, and title scale.
- Keep cards inside genuine background/monitor space.
- Move sparse keyword rhythm to the lower left.
- Add a subtle current-beat rail to balance the frame without creating another card.
- Use B-roll as a low-opacity breathing texture rather than constant full-screen interruption.

Lesson:

Balance does not mean equal weight. The speaker remains primary; secondary rails only prevent visual collapse.

## LongCat Skill Demo: Layout Survey And Repetition Check

What worked:

- Sampling ten source frames made a stable left support zone defensible.
- Emphasis near 29% preserved the speaker as trust layer.
- Unsupported claims stayed spoken judgments instead of fake proof.

Issue found:

- The first keyword-card implementation repeated the title in the header and body.

Lesson:

- Validate card text nodes for duplication.
- Test real Chinese technical terms after transcription.
- Check final renders for black frames as well as visible overlap.

## Reusable Deductions

- One screen, one primary visual is a hierarchy rule, not a minimalism slogan.
- A-track is not filler between effects; it is the source of trust and authority.
- Card variety should follow semantic variety.
- Typography must be designed before components are coded.
- Aspect ratio changes composition, not only scale.
- Generated media improves semantic fit only when it stays honest about being illustration.
- Contact sheets reveal hierarchy and repetition; motion spot checks reveal timing collisions.
- Every recurring review issue should become a validator, token, component, or reference rule.
