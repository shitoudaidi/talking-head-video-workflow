## Visual Character

Aim for practical, field-tested, judgment-heavy, clean but not cold.

The visual world should communicate:

- A real person tested or reasoned about something.
- The editor knows which sentence matters.
- Evidence and explanation are visually distinct.
- Motion is controlled enough to feel intentional.

Do not aim for generic "futuristic AI" decoration. Grids, glows, scanlines, and code textures need a structural job. Use a faint grid to stabilize a full-screen explainer stage; do not place random symbols behind a visible speaker.

## Hierarchy

Every frame must answer in this order:

1. What is the primary idea?
2. Is the speaker or the visual carrying it?
3. What supporting detail is optional?
4. Where does the eye rest after reading?

Use one primary visual. Supporting layers may coexist only when their zones and roles are distinct.

Good hierarchy examples:

- Speaker + bottom subtitle
- Speaker + one compact side card + subtitle
- Full-screen metric + small source label
- Proof panel + one instruction label + subtitle
- Final thesis + one underline or rule

Reject:

- Giant word + structured card + keyword chips + subtitle
- Two cards with equal visual weight
- Header, progress bar, beat rail, tags, and card all competing
- A card title that repeats its body or subtitle
- A large visual placed because the sentence contains a noun

## Surface Language

Use restrained geometry:

- Cards: 6-8px radius, thin border, one accent rail or indicator
- Proof panels: 8px radius, quiet frame, minimal label
- Pills: reserve for short metadata or states, not commands or headlines
- Shadows: broad and low-opacity; avoid glossy floating UI
- Accent light: local to the active element; do not wash the whole frame in one hue

Keep page-like sections unframed. Use cards only for actual structured units: comparison, metric, checklist, process, proof, or verdict.

## Color Semantics

- Blue: structure, capability, mechanism, positive conclusion
- Cyan: secondary technical signal or continuity
- Orange: conflict, warning, reversal, cost, skepticism
- Red: verified risk or failure only
- White: reading and the main claim
- Gray: translation, provenance, labels, secondary explanation

Do not use orange and blue at equal intensity everywhere. One accent leads each beat.

## Coherence Across Time

Keep these stable across the video:

- Font family and weight logic
- Border radius and border treatment
- Accent semantics
- Subtitle position
- Card entry direction for the same zone
- Metadata naming style

Create variety through semantic card shapes and timing, not unrelated visual styles.

Use a recurring visual anchor per section when explaining a mechanism. Let side information change around it instead of replacing the main object every two seconds.

## Aesthetic Rejection Rules

Reject a frame if:

- It looks like a template demo instead of a talking-head video.
- The point cannot be described after a one-second glance.
- Text is fragmented into too many boxes.
- All text levels look similar.
- The largest text is not the most important thought.
- A support layer is more visually intense than the proof or speaker.
- The frame duplicates the same sentence in headline, card, and subtitle.
- The frame would improve by removing an element.
- Adjacent beats change style without a semantic reason.
- Decoration is the only explanation for an animation.

## Three-Pass Review Rubric

Score each representative frame from 0-2.

| Dimension | 0 | 1 | 2 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Primary idea | unclear | discoverable | immediate |
| Speaker safety | blocked | tight | protected |
| Typography | weak/overflow | readable | clear hierarchy |
| Semantic fit | decorative | loosely related | transforms meaning |
| Visual balance | lopsided/noisy | acceptable | intentional |
| Coherence | foreign style | mostly related | same visual world |
| Proof honesty | misleading | ambiguous | clearly classified |

Any `0` fails the frame. A final contact sheet should average at least `1.7` with no zero.
